Task Modification: Environmental Action Plan (Geography / HASS)

Standards & Focus Areas:

Std 1 — 1.5 1.6 Std 2 — 2.3 Std 3 — 3.5 Std 5 — 5.3
Claim:

I modified an existing “Environmental Change and Management” report task into a structured Environmental Action Plan scaffold to better support students with diverse learning needs, particularly around literacy, organisation, and task clarity.

Evidence:

Original BHS task sheet outlining the full Environmental Change and Management report (research plan + 800–1000 word report) and a simplified Environmental Action Plan version with clear headings, sentence stems, and step-by-step prompts.

Context:

Geography/HASS class completing an Environmental Change and Management unit. The original task required an extended written report and independent research. Several students were struggling with reading load, task complexity, and knowing how to structure their ideas. The modified Environmental Action Plan breaks the task into small, explicit sections: Introduction, Regional Overview, Stakeholders, Environmental Issues, Environmental Management, Recommendations, and Conclusion, with simple prompts and examples.

Evaluate:

This modification makes expectations more visible and reduces cognitive load by chunking the task and providing sentence starters (e.g. “Write a simple opening statement about the location you have chosen”; “List the people who care about this place and why”). It is designed to help students who need more support with reading and writing to participate meaningfully in the same assessment task. In future iterations, I will:

  • Co-create an example Environmental Action Plan with the class to model quality responses.
  • Offer two tiers of scaffold (high support and medium support) so students can gradually move towards more independence.
  • Build in a quick formative checkpoint (e.g. short draft of “Issues” + “Recommendations”) so I can check understanding and adjust teaching before the final submission.

Artifact List:

  • Original Environmental Change and Management task sheet (PDF)
  • Modified Environmental Action Plan scaffold (Doc/PDF)

Impact (to refine with your own notes/data):

  • Students have a clearer sequence of headings and prompts to follow, reducing “I don’t know where to start.”
  • The scaffold explicitly names stakeholders, issues, management, and recommendations, supporting content vocabulary.
  • You can now observe and compare how students respond using the scaffold vs. the original open-ended report format.

Next Steps (linked to Focus Areas):

  • 1.5 / 1.6: Develop additional versions of the scaffold (word banks, visuals, and exemplars) tailored to specific learner groups.
  • 2.3: Add explicit links back to curriculum language (e.g. “ecosystem services”, “sustainability”) within each section.
  • 3.5 / 5.3: Use students’ draft sections (“Issues” and “Recommendations”) as a formative check to adjust teaching and provide feedback before the final submission.

Year 9 Geography — Food Security Portfolio Assessment

Std 11.21.5 Std 22.12.2 Std 33.13.2 Std 44.14.2 Std 55.25.5 Std 66.2 Std 77.3

Claim: I introduced a portfolio-based assessment in Year 9 Geography (Food Security unit) to increase student engagement, make classwork meaningful, and provide continuous assessment instead of relying on one or two large summative tasks. The portfolio breaks the term’s work into weekly tasks that all contribute to the overall grade, giving students clear goals and a sense that everyday effort matters.

Evidence: The evidence set includes the written “Year 9 Portfolio Assessment” explanation, the tracking documents (e.g. Week 1 totals), and the Year 9 grades spreadsheet. Together they show how weekly portfolio tasks were structured and scored, how groups were organised, and how student progress was monitored over time.

Context: In this class I noticed that students often treated ordinary classwork as unimportant because only a small number of summative assessments “counted”. This led to disengagement and incomplete work. The portfolio approach was designed to respond to this by making classwork visible and consequential. Each weekly task was worth 20 marks and contributed to the final grade. Students worked in groups of four, with a prize for the group with the highest average, to encourage collaboration, peer accountability, and positive competition.

Evaluate: The portfolio assessment improved student engagement and accountability. Students became more active participants in lessons, took weekly tasks more seriously, and showed greater persistence in completing work. Group structures and the prize system created healthy competition and encouraged students to support each other. The tracking documents and grades also allowed me to communicate clearly with parents about progress and missing work, which often resulted in parents encouraging students to complete outstanding tasks at home.

Reflecting on this approach, I can see that it successfully highlighted the importance of continuous assessment and regular feedback. However, introducing it in Term 4 meant there was an adjustment period while students learned the expectations and deadlines. In future, I would introduce the portfolio earlier in the year, explain the system more explicitly (including time-management strategies), and explore different group structures or incentives to sustain motivation across the term.

How this meets the Standards:

  • Std 1.2 & 1.5: The portfolio and group structure acknowledge different learning needs and provide opportunities for students to engage in ways that suit their abilities, with scope for modification and support.
  • Std 2.1 & 2.2: Breaking Food Security content into weekly tasks shows deliberate selection and organisation of content into manageable, engaging chunks.
  • Std 3.1 & 3.2: Weekly, points-based tasks set clear and challenging goals and demonstrate structured planning across the term.
  • Std 4.1 & 4.2: Group work and weekly monitoring support participation and help manage classroom activity so students stay involved.
  • Std 5.2 & 5.5: Regular marking of weekly tasks provides ongoing feedback, and the grade tracking sheet supports consistent reporting to students and parents.
  • Std 6.2: Designing and trialling this new assessment method shows reflection on practice and a commitment to improving student engagement.
  • Std 7.3: The portfolio data and grade tracking support meaningful communication with parents about student progress, deadlines, and achievement.
